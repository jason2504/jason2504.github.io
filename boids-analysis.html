Boids Algorithm Analysis

Performance Analysis of Boids Algorithm in Coverage Scenarios

Key Achievement

Conducted 48,000 systematic simulations across 2,000 gain vectors, achieving 85.76% area coverage in complex cafeteria environments. Research revealed that minimal alignment forces (k_ali = 0.003) with moderate cohesion (k_coh = 0.213) yield optimal coverage performance, challenging conventional flocking wisdom.

Project Overview

This MEAM 6240 Distributed Robotics project investigated the effectiveness of the classical boids flocking algorithm for area coverage tasks. Through comprehensive simulation analysis, we systematically explored how different parameter combinations affect swarm coverage performance in complex environments, providing insights crucial for real-world distributed robotics applications.

Research Methodology

This work systematically analyzed the boids model for distributed coverage tasks through extensive parameter optimization. We implemented a custom PyGame-based simulator supporting both interactive and headless modes, enabling systematic exploration of the relationship between flocking parameters and coverage performance across diverse environments.

Boids Algorithm Fundamentals

The boids algorithm simulates the flocking behavior of birds through three simple rules that create emergent complexity from individual interactions. Each boid follows local rules of separation, alignment, and cohesion to produce realistic group behavior without centralized control.

The three fundamental boids behaviors: Separation (avoid crowding), Alignment (steer toward average heading), and Cohesion (move toward average position of neighbors).

Simulation Framework

Agents are modeled as kinematic point masses with bounded acceleration, executing a prioritized behavior stack: obstacle avoidance → separation → alignment → cohesion → target seeking. This prioritization ensures collision-avoidance consumes control authority before lower-priority objectives, preventing pathological force superposition found in naive vector summation approaches.

The core behavioral forces are mathematically formulated as:

  • Separation: fcol = kcol Σ(xi - xj) for j ∈ Ni
  • Alignment: fali = kali (⟨ẋj⟩ - ẋi)
  • Cohesion: fcoh = kcoh (⟨xj⟩ - xi)

Each simulation ran for 60 seconds at 60 Hz (3,600 timesteps) in an 800×600 pixel domain. The headless optimization mode supported parallel evaluation using Python's multiprocessing module, enabling efficient parameter space exploration across multiple random seeds.

Coverage Task and Metrics

The primary objective is spatial coverage: maximizing the fraction of free workspace area visited at least once by any agent. A binary coverage mask is updated each frame by inflating agent positions by a small footprint radius. To measure efficiency of exploration, we compute the coefficient of variation (CV) of visit counts per pixel. High coverage with low CV corresponds to rapid and uniform exploration; high coverage with high CV indicates redundant retracing.

The benchmark set contains four environments with different constraints: a dense cafeteria layout, a standard cafeteria, a narrow corridor with a bottleneck, and an empty arena. These span heavy clutter to free space and expose different failure modes such as congestion at chokepoints or poor utilization of occluded regions.

Parameter Optimization

We conducted a random search across 2,000 gain vectors (ksep, kali, kcoh), repeated under multiple random seeds, yielding 48,000 simulations in total when scaled across environments and swarm sizes. Early sweeps indicated that large alignment weights consistently degraded coverage, motivating subsequent sweeps with restricted alignment ranges. All other simulation parameters were fixed to isolate the effect of relative gain weighting.

Key Findings

For N=50 and N=100 agents, coverage increased monotonically with swarm size across all environments. In cluttered maps, larger swarms also reduced variance in visitation, overcoming bottlenecks more effectively. Our results reveal several critical insights:

  • Alignment Paradox: Alignment displayed a strong negative correlation with coverage beyond kali ≈ 0.03, as high-alignment swarms collapse into rigid flocks that underutilize free space
  • Optimal Configuration: In the cafeteria environment with 100 agents, the optimal configuration (kcoh, kali, ksep) = (0.213, 0.003, 0.003) achieved mean coverage of 85.8% across seeds with low CV
  • Spatial Dispersion: Moderate cohesion and separation improved spatial dispersion without overconstraining motion
  • Bottleneck Sensitivity: Corridor scenarios highlight sensitivity to bottlenecks - insufficient separation prevents agents from diffusing into downstream regions

Technical Implementation

Simulation Architecture

  • Prioritized behavior stack with acceleration budget (0.5 units/timestep)
  • Bounded velocity model (max speed: 5 units)
  • Local sensing: 50-unit radius, 150° field of view
  • Wall repulsion with inverse-distance fields (k_wall = 10)
  • 2-pixel agent footprint for coverage calculation

Optimization Process

  • Random search across 2,000 gain vectors
  • Three random seeds per configuration (27, 729, 4913)
  • Gain ranges: k_coh ∈ [0.0, 0.5], k_ali ∈ [0.0, 0.1], k_col ∈ [0.0, 0.5]
  • Parallel evaluation using multiprocessing
  • Both N=50 and N=100 agent configurations tested

Coverage Metrics and Analysis

Coverage percentage was defined as the ratio of unique visited pixels to total traversable pixels. We also computed the coefficient of variation (CV) from heatmap visitation frequencies to measure exploration uniformity: CV = σ/μ, where lower values indicate more uniform spatial distribution. This dual-metric approach ensured evaluation of both coverage extent and exploration efficiency.

Optimal Configurations by Environment

  • Cafeteria (N=100): (0.213, 0.003, 0.003) → 85.76% coverage
  • Dense Cafeteria (N=100): (0.477, 0.017, 0.001) → 51.95% coverage
  • Narrow Corridor (N=100): (0.070, 0.010, 0.001) → 76.36% coverage
  • Empty Arena (N=100): (0.223, 0.001, 0.004) → 96.81% coverage

Key Performance Insights

  • Coverage increased monotonically with swarm size (N=50 → N=100)
  • Alignment exhibited strong negative correlation beyond k_ali > 0.03
  • Larger swarms reduced variance in cluttered environments
  • CV uniformity: Empty (0.44) > Cafeteria (0.56) > Corridor (0.82) > Dense (0.98)
  • Bottleneck sensitivity required sufficient separation for agent diffusion

Technical Discussion and Implications

Our results demonstrate that local boid rules, when tuned for task performance rather than biological fidelity, provide a lightweight distributed controller for coverage tasks. The prioritized-acceleration formulation prevents pathological force superposition while yielding interpretable behavior. Optimal coverage emerges with negligible alignment, moderate cohesion, and minimal separation—effectively encouraging dispersion while preserving group coherency.

Heatmap analysis confirms that low-alignment swarms spread broadly and exploit occluded regions, while high-alignment swarms collapse into rigid flocks that underutilize free space. The framework's limitation lies in idealized sensing assumptions and simplified dynamics, though large-scale randomized sweeps enabled principled characterization of performance landscapes infeasible in hardware.
