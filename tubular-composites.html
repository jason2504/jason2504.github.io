Project Overview

Tubular braided composites (TBCs) represent a critical class of advanced materials with widespread applications in aerospace and automotive industries due to their exceptional strength-to-weight ratio and complex loading capabilities. This comprehensive research project, conducted under the University of Alberta Dean Research Award with Dr. Ahmed Samir Ead, focused on the mechanical characterization of EPON 826/LS-81K epoxy matrix systems that serve as the binding agent distributing loads and providing cohesion to the overall composite structure.

Three-Phase Research Methodology

The investigation was systematically structured into three primary research phases, each targeting specific mechanical properties essential for understanding TBC failure mechanisms and achieving optimal performance characteristics. This methodical approach enabled comprehensive material characterization from fundamental properties to advanced thermal behavior.

Phase 1: Torsional Properties Investigation

The initial research focus centered on determining the shear strength and shear modulus of EPON 826/LS-81K through controlled torsional testing. This work was motivated by literature findings suggesting that epoxy resins may exhibit ductile failure modes under torsional loads, contrasting with their typically brittle tensile behavior. Understanding these properties was crucial for predicting TBC failure modes under compression, where shear strength often becomes the limiting factor determining overall composite performance.

The testing protocol involved systematic sample preparation with 50/50 epoxy-to-hardener mixing ratios, controlled curing in steel molds, and application of speckle patterns for Digital Image Correlation analysis. Using the Torsion Master testing system combined with advanced DIC stereo camera systems, we captured real-time shear strain data (Î³_xy) during torsional loading, enabling direct measurement of shear modulus and identification of failure mechanisms.

Phase 2: Compressive Properties Characterization

The second phase involved comprehensive compression testing to determine the compressive Young's modulus and validate material isotropy. Using the Instron TTKH testing machine with integrated DIC analysis, results demonstrated that the compressive Young's modulus (E = 2640 Â± 270 MPa) closely matched the literature tensile modulus (E = 2730 MPa), providing strong evidence for material isotropy - a fundamental assumption critical for composite analysis models.

The testing revealed that epoxy exhibits ductile yielding under compression while maintaining brittle fracture characteristics, providing crucial insights for composite failure prediction. Stress-strain analysis showed a clear linear elastic region followed by ductile yielding, contrasting with the brittle tensile failure mode documented in literature.

Phase 3: Thermal Expansion Analysis

The final phase investigated the thermal expansion behavior of optimized composite systems, ultimately achieving near-zero coefficient of thermal expansion - a remarkable property for aerospace applications where dimensional stability across temperature ranges is critical. This achievement demonstrates the potential for thermally stable composite structures that maintain geometric integrity under varying environmental conditions encountered in aerospace and high-performance applications.

The thermal expansion research involved systematic optimization of braid angles (35Â° to 55Â°), fiber selection (Kevlar prepregs), and manufacturing parameters using a maypole braiding machine. Through iterative design and testing, optimal configurations were identified that balanced mechanical performance with thermal stability requirements.

Technical Innovation and Advanced Methods

Digital Image Correlation (DIC) Implementation

Advanced stereo camera systems with DaVis software enabled real-time full-field strain measurement during both torsional and compression testing. The DIC methodology involved systematic speckle pattern application, precise camera calibration, and synchronized data acquisition with mechanical testing machines. This approach provided unprecedented insight into material deformation behavior and failure mechanisms.

Results Analysis and Impact

Material Property Validation

The research successfully validated EPON 826/LS-81K as an isotropic, homogeneous material through direct comparison of tensile and compressive moduli. The close agreement between compressive modulus (2640 Â± 270 MPa) and literature tensile values (2730 MPa) confirms fundamental assumptions used in composite analysis models.

Failure Mode Characterization

The discovery of different failure modes under tension (brittle) versus compression (ductile yielding with brittle fracture) provides critical insights for composite design. This understanding enables more accurate failure prediction and optimization of composite structures for specific loading conditions.

Aerospace Application Impact

Achievement of near-zero coefficient of thermal expansion represents a significant advancement for aerospace applications. This property enables dimensional stability across the extreme temperature ranges encountered in aerospace environments, reducing thermal stress and improving structural reliability.
