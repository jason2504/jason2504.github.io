Tubular Braided Composites

With Near-Zero Thermal Expansion

Project Overview

This research project, conducted under the University of Alberta Dean Research Award, focused on developing tubular braided composites with near-zero coefficient of thermal expansion for aerospace applications. The work involved comprehensive characterization of epoxy resin properties and optimization of manufacturing parameters to achieve dimensional stability across temperature ranges.

Research Objectives

  • Characterize compressive properties of epoxy resin under various loading conditions
  • Investigate failure modes using digital image correlation (DIC) analysis
  • Optimize braid angles and epoxy-hardener ratios for thermal stability
  • Validate manufacturing process for consistent material properties

Technical Approach

The research employed a systematic approach to composite manufacturing and characterization:

  • Manufacturing: Utilized maypole braiding machine with Kevlar prepregs, systematically varying braid angles from 35° to 55°
  • Material Optimization: Investigated various epoxy-hardener ratios to achieve optimal thermal and mechanical properties
  • Characterization: Employed DaVis software and 2D stereo camera systems for precise strain analysis and thermal deformation tracking
  • Validation: Conducted extensive testing to validate epoxy isotropy and homogeneity

Key Findings

The research successfully achieved near-zero coefficient of thermal expansion composites through iterative design optimization. Key discoveries included:

  • Demonstration of different failure modes under tension (brittle) versus compression (ductile)
  • Optimal braid angle ranges for thermal stability
  • Validation of manufacturing consistency across multiple samples
  • Correlation between microstructure and thermal expansion properties
